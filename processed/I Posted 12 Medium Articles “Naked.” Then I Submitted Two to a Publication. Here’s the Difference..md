## TL;DR: In 12 days, I published 12 Medium articles. Total Reads across all of them: 4. I had never submitted a single one to a publication. Two weeks after I figured that out, I had submitted exactly two articles to one publication. The numbers tell a story I wish I’d seen earlier.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*Tq-l9vhBtZOqyfJA6kvq6Q.png)

### I’m a solo founder. I’m also a 25-year engineer with zero marketing background.

My Product Hunt launch was on April 15. I started writing on Medium about a week later — first article went up on April 22.

Two weeks and 12 articles later, here’s where the numbers stood (as of May 7):

\- 12 articles published  
\- 164 total presentations (times shown in feeds)  
\- 33 total views  
\- 4 total reads  
\- 58 total claps  
\- \*\*0 publications submitted to\*\*

Two articles (“X Banned Me During My Product Hunt Launch” and “2 Weeks After Product Hunt: The Numbers Nobody Shows You”) got 21 claps each. The other ten got close to nothing. I assumed the two that worked were “good,” and the rest were “not good enough.”

That was wrong. The real answer was simpler — and more embarrassing.

### What I missed

Medium has a distribution mechanism called publications. They’re curated collections, each with their own follower base. When an article gets accepted into a publication, it appears in those followers’ feeds.

Publishing on Medium without submitting to any publication is, roughly, like:

- Hitting “deploy” on a server with no DNS
- Sending an email with no recipients
- Launching on Product Hunt without scheduling the launch

The article exists. It’s technically “published.” Practically, almost no one finds it.

I did this 12 times.

> 164 presentations → 33 views → 4 reads

In two weeks. Across 12 articles. With real effort behind each one.

### How I missed it

Two reasons, both on me.

**One: engineer brain.** When I press “Publish,” I assume I’ve shipped — the way I deploy a feature, get a green CI, and trust the system to route requests. I didn’t realize “publish” on Medium just creates the artifact. Distribution is a second, manual step.

**Two: someone had explicitly opened the door for me, and I stood next to it.**

On April 27, an editor at Startup Stash messaged me about one of my articles (“I Almost Paid $1,250 for DR”). They said they liked it. They said their readers would love it. They had already added me as a writer to their publication. They told me that if I submitted the article, they’d push it through their social and newsletters.

I replied: “Thank you so much! I’ll add this piece to Startup Stash and have more articles coming soon.”

Then I did nothing. For ten days.

Being added as a writer just gives you the \*ability\* to submit. To actually submit, you have to open each article’s menu, click “Submit to publication,” choose the publication, and wait for the editor to review and accept it.

I never did that step. I had replied to the message and stopped there. Someone had opened a door. I just stood next to it.

### What happened when I finally walked through

On May 7, I submitted “I Almost Paid $1,250 for DR” to Startup Stash. Five days later (May 12), it got approved.

Startup Stash has 16.6K followers. For the first time, my article appeared in their feeds.

For the second article — “I Listed on 30+ Directories. My DR Climbed to 21. Then Stopped” — I tried a different approach. I drafted it as a Medium story, but instead of publishing it on my own, I submitted it directly to Startup Stash and waited. About a week later, on May 20, the editor approved it and it went live in the publication’s feed on the same day it became visible on my profile.

Two articles. That’s it. Two menu clicks I had been avoiding for weeks.

Here’s the difference, side by side. Same author. Same writing voice. The variable that changed was two menu clicks.

The subscriber is the part I keep staring at.

A subscriber on Medium means someone is asking to get an email every time I publish. After 19 articles’ worth of effort and zero subscribers, one arrived in the two-week window where two of my articles were running through a publication.

I can’t prove the subscriber came from the publication. But I know they didn’t come from naked posting, because naked posting had produced exactly zero in the month before.

### The two articles, individually

The two Startup Stash submissions also stand out inside the May numbers:

| - -| - -| - -| - -| - -|
| #5 "I Almost Paid $1,250 for DR" (submitted May 7, approved May 12) | Apr 26 | **115** | 7 | 1 |

Two submitted articles = 309 presentations.  
Seventeen naked articles in the same month = 258 presentations combined.

Two articles outperformed seventeen articles. By menu click.

### Naked ≠ Invisible

To be fair to naked publishing: outside traffic still happens. A few of my articles actually have more views than presentations — meaning more people arrived from somewhere \*outside\* Medium than Medium’s own algorithm sent in.

```c
| Article | Presentations | Views | Where the extra came from |
| - -| - -| - -| - -|
| I Launched My SaaS on Product Hunt | 16 | 29 | Product Hunt links, X share |
| Your Claude Code Automations Are About to Get a Bill | 13 | 21 | X share, external search |
```

So nakedness doesn’t mean zero. It just means **zero Medium-internal distribution.** Every view above came from somewhere I had pushed traffic **to** — my X account, my Product Hunt page, a search result.

External traffic is one vector. Publication submission is a different one. The X follower count doesn’t move when I click “Submit to publication.” But the feed exposure inside Medium does — and that’s the layer naked publishing leaves locked.

I’d been doing one of two vectors. Submitting to a publication adds the other.

### The lesson I keep relearning

I built distribution into my SaaS. Pricing pages. SEO. Sitemaps. canonical tags. Backlinks. Search Console. I sweated every layer.

I forgot to build distribution into my writing.

Content without distribution is a private journal with extra steps.

When I finally submitted, I thought “get into feeds” was the whole problem. It wasn’t. Distribution is layered:

1. **Get into feeds** → solved by publication submission
2. **Get clicks from feeds** → moderate (309 presentations produced 18 views)
3. **Get reads from views** → working better than expected on the second submission (8 reads on 11 views)
4. **Get follows / subscribers from reads** → finally moved (+1 subscriber, first one ever)

Each layer only becomes visible once you’ve cleared the one below. I couldn’t see layer 4 until layer 1 was solved.

### If you’re me, two weeks ago

If you’re a solo founder posting on Medium and you haven’t submitted any of your articles to a publication, I’d stop reading and go check.

The fix is one menu click per article. The cost of not doing it is roughly everything I lost in April.

A thank-you, while I’m here: to the Startup Stash editor who messaged me on April 27 and waited ten days for a reply that didn’t come, then accepted my submission when it finally did — the door you opened changed the shape of my numbers. I should have walked through sooner.

I have 17 more naked articles in my profile. Going back to submit each one isn’t the answer. Making submission the default for the next article is.
