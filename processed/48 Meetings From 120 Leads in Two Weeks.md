## The email was the last step. The unlock was timing.

A couple of weeks ago we booked 48 meetings from 120 leads. Not 48 replies. 48 meetings, in two weeks.

The old version of me would have turned that into a post about AI outbound or personalisation at scale. But the more I pulled it apart, the more obvious it became that this had almost nothing to do with the email itself. The email was the last step. The actual unlock was deciding when an email should exist in the first place.

Most outbound teams are still solving the wrong problem. They ask how to send more emails, faster, with better personalisation, across more channels. The better question is what just happened that makes this account worth contacting right now.

That is where signals come in.

### Volume stopped being the moat

For years outbound was a volume game. Buy a list, enrich it, segment it, write a few sequences, rotate inboxes, drop in a personalised opener, push more people through. That worked when inboxes were emptier and buyers had less AI-generated noise hitting them every week.

Now everyone has the same tools. Everyone can scrape. Everyone can enrich. Everyone can generate a “personalised” line in two seconds. The copy is not the advantage anymore. The advantage is knowing who to contact, why now, and what context actually matters when you reach out. Different motion entirely.

### Here’s the flow we built

Step one is a focused list of target accounts. That can sit in HubSpot, Attio, Salesforce, or just a CSV. The CRM does not really matter. What matters is that you are not starting with 50,000 random contacts hoping volume saves you. You are starting with companies you actually want to win.

Step two is configuring the signals we watch inside Trigify. These are public events that suggest an account or person is worth acting on right now. The ones we use most:

→ competitor engagement on LinkedIn or X

→ hiring intent in specific GTM roles

→ founder or operator posts about a problem we solve

→ funding news

→ pain-point mentions in comments

→ product launches in adjacent categories

→ people publicly asking for recommendations

That becomes the trigger layer. Instead of asking “who is in our database”, the system asks “what just happened that we should pay attention to”. Sounds like a small distinction. It changes the entire outbound motion.

Step three is where Claude Code does the work behind the scenes. When a signal fires, the workflow runs in eight steps — enrich the person, find the email, research the person and the company, read the exact signal that fired, draft the personalised email with that context, push the lead into Instantly, ping the team in Slack with the reasoning.

The important part is that Claude is not writing cold emails in a vacuum. It has the signal. It knows what happened, who it happened to, and which signal type it is. That is the difference between fake personalisation and useful context.

### The email is becoming the commodity

This is the bit most people miss. The email itself is the commodity layer now. AI can write a decent email. It can match your tone. It can shorten it, sharpen it, reference the company, mention the role. Fine.

But if the person has no reason to care today, the email is still just another interruption. The hard part is not generating the message. The hard part is knowing when the message has a reason to exist.

Someone engaging with a competitor today is not the same as someone sitting cold in your CRM. Someone hiring for a GTM role this week is not the same as a company that matched your ICP 18 months ago. Someone publicly complaining about a problem you solve is not the same as a generic lead with the right job title.

Most teams are still not set up to catch those moments.

### Why this actually worked

The 48 meetings did not come from a magic template. They came from a better filter. We were not trying to contact everyone. We were trying to contact the right people, after the right public event, with enough context that the message felt obvious to the person reading it.

The shift is:

→ less list blasting, more signal monitoring

→ less “personalisation at scale”, more actual context

→ less guessing, more timing

→ less volume, better reasons

The next wave of outbound is going to look less like a traditional SDR motion and more like a signal operating system. The CRM still matters. The email tool still matters. Enrichment still matters. But they become downstream actions. The upstream layer is the signal: what happened, who did it happen to, is it relevant, is the timing strong, what should happen next.

### Signal, context, action

That’s the simplest way to think about it.

Signal is the thing that happened. A competitor engagement. A hiring post. A pain-point mention. A funding announcement. A founder publicly talking about the problem you solve. A buyer asking for recommendations.

Context is the judgement layer. Who is this person, what company, are they in our ICP, why does this event matter, is it worth acting on right now.

Action is the workflow. Research the person, draft the message, push into Instantly, route to Slack, add to CRM, create a task, alert the rep. Whatever makes sense for that signal type.

Most outbound skips the first two layers and jumps straight to action. That is why it feels noisy.

### Where this goes

My bet for 2026 is that outbound will not be won by the teams sending the most emails. It will be won by the teams with the best signal infrastructure. The teams that know when an account is warming up before it ever fills in a form. The teams that can spot real intent in public conversations, competitor engagement, hiring patterns, community posts, and founder activity. The teams that can route that context into action quickly without turning it into spam.

The lazy version of AI outbound is the one where the model writes 10,000 emails. The better version is the one we are running now. AI watches the market. AI reads the signal. AI adds the context. AI helps decide the next action. Then a human or a workflow acts when there is an actual reason to.

It’s a much more interesting system to operate. It’s also a much better experience for the buyer, which is the part everyone forgets.

Moral of the story: 48 meetings from 120 leads in two weeks was not a volume play. It was a timing play. If there is no signal, do not send the email. If there is a signal, understand it properly before you act.

Less volume. Better reasons. That is where outbound is going.
