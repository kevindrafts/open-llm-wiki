One night I noticed something.

A guy posted:

> “Why do I look different in every photo?”

Another guy posted:

> “Is my jaw recessed?”

Another guy posted twelve photos of himself from twelve angles like he was applying for a position as a surveillance camera.

Same questions.

Same confusion.

Every day.

Thousands of people.

And somehow nobody was packaging the answers.

So I made a PDF.

- Not a book.
- Not a course.
- Not a masterclass taught by a guy standing next to a rented Lamborghini.

**Just a PDF.**

A small ugly PDF explaining looksmaxing basics in plain English.

The kind of thing you could read before your microwave finished heating leftovers.

**I threw together a faceless TikTok account.**

- No face.
- No personal brand.
- No inspirational sunrise videos.

No “Hey guys, welcome back to my channel.”

Just simple videos answering questions people were already asking.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!31Jx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F57284d58-ab9b-4863-83c8-a83c5f5b351c_720x1372.jpeg)

### That’s the part most people miss.

I wasn’t creating demand.

**The demand was already there.**

People were literally begging for answers.

I just stopped the addicting scrolling long enough to notice.

A funny thing happens when you answer a question someone is already obsessing over.

They pay attention.

A guy bought the PDF.

$19.99.

**Honestly, I was shocked.**

Not because of the money.

Because a real human being (saw something I made) and thought:

> “Yeah. This solves my problem.”

---

### That first sale feels weird.

You expect fireworks.

Instead you stare at the notification for fifteen minutes like a confused caveman discovering fire.

Then another person bought.

Then another.

Then another.

Eventually 301 people bought it.

Which sounds impressive until I tell you the business model was basically:

1. **Notice question.**
2. **Answer question.**
3. **Stop overcomplicating question.**

That’s it.

- No funnel.
- No webinar.
- No seventeen-email sequence pretending to be your friend.

Just useful information packaged better than the average Reddit comment.

---

### And that’s when I realized there are three kinds of people using AI right now.

#### The first kind treats ChatGPT like a slot machine.

They pull the lever.

Get an answer.

Copy.

Paste.

Publish.

Then wonder why nobody cares.

Because everyone can smell AI slop now.

It’s like microwave fish.

You know immediately.

---

#### The second kind treats ChatGPT like a PhD advisor.

They spend six weeks researching.

Thirty-seven tabs open.

Seven notebooks.

Four prompt libraries.

Zero products.

They know everything except how to click Publish.

---

#### The third kind does something different.

They use ChatGPT like an employee.

A smart employee.

A fast employee.

An employee who never sleeps.

But still an employee.

1. They tell it what to do.
2. They edit.
3. They simplify.
4. They remove the garbage.
5. They keep the useful stuff.
6. And then they ship.

Those people make money.

Not because they’re smarter.

Because they’re faster.

**Most people think business is about intelligence.**

**A lot of the time it’s just noticing painfully obvious things before everyone else does.**

Like:

> “Wow. Thousands of guys/girls seem confused about their appearance.”

Or:

> “Wow. People keep asking the exact same question.”

Or:

> “Wow. Maybe I should package this instead of endlessly consuming content about packaging it.”

Revolutionary stuff.

I know.

The best part?

The PDF wasn’t perfect.

Looking back, some pages make me physically uncomfortable.

A few graphics looked like they survived a small house fire.

The formatting had the confidence of a substitute teacher on the first day.

**People bought it anyway.**

**Because buyers care way less about perfection than creators do.**
