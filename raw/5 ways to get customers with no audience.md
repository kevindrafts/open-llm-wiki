Building an audience is a long-term strategy.  
Building a business is a short-term survival game.

While building an audience is one of the best investments a founder can make, the problem is that investments take time to pay off.

And most startups in early stage don't have unlimited time.

- Bills arrive monthly.
- Runway gets shorter every day.
- There are expenses to cover.
- There are assumptions to test.

That’s why many founders get stuck.

They spend their early days optimizing for attention when what they really need is traction.

After all…

→ A founder with 10 customers and no audience has a business.

→ A founder with 10,000 followers and no customers just has an audience.

So, *“How do I get customers before anyone knows who I am?,”* you asked.

That’s the question **Rob Walling** was answering.

***Subscribe, become a member, or gift a subscription to a friend.***

### Some context

**Rob Walling** has started 6 companies, 5 of them bootstrapped. He built and sold **Drip** for eight figures, and created the infrastructure behind **MicroConf** and **TinySeed**, which today supports over 329 bootstrapped SaaS founders.

Rob Walling has spent two decades watching founders make the same mistake:

→ Treating audience-building as a prerequisite for selling, when the two can happen simultaneously and often should happen in the opposite order.

In his podcast, Startups for the Rest of Us, Rob Walling answered a listener question specifically about finding early customers without an audience. The question came from a pre-revenue founder who felt stuck waiting until they had a following before approaching customers.

Rob Walling’s response was direct:

→ You don’t need an audience to get your first customers. You need (1) a specific person with (2) a specific problem and (3) a direct way to reach them.

### Your first 10 customers are already in front of you!

Here are Rob Walling’s 5 ways to find your first customers TODAY when you have no audience:

#### 1\. Go where your customers already are.

**Greg Mercer**, the founder of **Jungle Scout**, got his first sale in 3-4 weeks by answering questions in Facebook groups.

The audience already exists.

Your job in the early days is not to build a new one but to find the rooms where your future customers are already talking and become genuinely useful inside them.

**This is your sign to:**

- Go on Reddit and offer helpful advice to complaints
- Go into Facebook groups and answer questions
- Go on LinkedIn and engage with discussions about the pain point

Your first customer is probably already talking about their problem somewhere online.

The question is whether you’re listening.

#### 2\. Cold email with one clear line of personalization.

**Josh Haynam**, the founder of **Interact**, sent 1,200 cold emails to land his first customers.

**Robi Ganguly**, the founder of **Appetitive**, sent 25 to 100 emails a day for months to build early pipeline.

Neither had an audience.

Both had *a specific person* they were trying to help and *a direct way* to say so.

**Here’s a simple cold email framework that works:**

- One line proving you know who they are
- One sentence describing the problem
- One question to validate the pain
- One FREE offer so they can try your product/service immediately
- One request for quick feedback

```markup
Use this template:

“Hi [Name],

Saw that you [personalized observation about their role, company, or recent activity].

I’ve been speaking with [target customer type] who struggle with [specific problem].

Is this something you’re dealing with today?

To test this idea, I put together a FREE [resource/audit/prototype/tool] that helps with [outcome]. You can access it here: [link].

I’d appreciate any quick feedback after trying it, even if it’s just one thing you liked, disliked, or found confusing.

Thanks,
[Your Name]”
```

Don’t ask for a meeting.  
Don’t ask for a demo.

Give away something useful first and ask for feedback.

The goal isn’t to close a sale.  
The goal is to start a conversation.

---

#### 3\. Put up a landing page before you have a product.

Rob Walling’s approach when starting out was to:

1. get a landing page up early
2. capture emails
3. reach out directly to ask what people were looking for.

The page does not need to be polished.

Just make sure it answers one question: *is anyone searching for this?*

**Here’s a simple landing page that works:**

```markup
Headline:
[Specific outcome].

Subheadline:
[How it works] + [Who it’s for]

CTA:
"Join the waitlist"
"Get early access"
"Be the first to try it"
```

Example 1:

- Headline — Get your first 10 customers in a week.
- Subheadline — A practical outreach playbook for founders starting from zero.
- CTA: Be the first to try it

Example 2:

- Headline — Build a business in 8 minutes.
- Subheadline — Turn your expertise into an AI-powered product without coding.
- CTA: Get early access

You’re not testing the product.  
You’re testing whether people want the outcome badly enough to leave their email.

#### 4\. Pre-sell before you write a line of code.

**Pete Koomen**, co-founder of **Optimizely**, pre-sold customers before writing a single line of code by running live demos of a prototype.

A working demo of a non-existent product is *still* a working demo.

If someone gives you money for it, the product is worth building.

**It’s only take you one day:**

- Mock up the most basic version of your product in Figma.
- Record a 3-minute demo video using Loom.
- Show it to 10 potential customers.
- Ask one question: *”If this existed today, would you pay for it?”*

A prototype costs hours.  
A wrong product costs months.

#### 5\. Build your network, not your audience.

Rob Walling’s counterintuitive take:

→ Building your network is almost always a better use of time than building an audience.

Audiences plateau.  
Networks compound.

10 warm introductions from people who trust you will outperform 10,000 followers who don’t really know you.

**Here’s how you do it:**

1. Pick ONE platform. (LinkedIn, X, etc)
2. Pick 10 people in your industry.
3. Spend 3-4 weeks consistently showing up in their world.
	- Comment thoughtfully on their posts.
		- Share useful ideas, introductions, or resources when relevant.
		- Start conversations around problems you're both interested in solving.

Don’t ask for anything.

Focus on becoming familiar, helpful, and memorable.

The goal is to become one introduction away from your next customer, hire, advisor, or opportunity.

### Thoughts

The one pattern that shows up across hundreds of founders who found early traction is this:

→ They did it through direct, personal conversations.

Not ads.  
Not viral loops.  
Not clever funnels.

It’s one-to-one outreach to people they believed had the problem they were solving.

An audience is a shortcut to distribution, but you don’t need distribution to get your first 10 customers.

All you need are a list of names, a specific problem, and a reason to reach out today instead of waiting.
