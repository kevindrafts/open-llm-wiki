*Everyone’s Teaching You to Build a SaaS App. Nobody’s Teaching You to Sell One.Here’s the Playbook I Wish Someone Had Handed Me.*

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:1400/format:webp/1*d4e4lr1YusoLbpqX4Kj4Zw.png)

Building a SaaS app has never been this easy. Describe what you want, an AI tool spits out a working product, you bolt on a payment gateway in an afternoon, and suddenly you’re “a founder.” YouTube is full of tutorials on exactly how to do that part.

> What almost nobody covers is the part that actually matters: getting a stranger to pull out their credit card and pay you. Search “how to build a SaaS app” and you’ll drown in results. Search “how to get customers for a SaaS app” and the well runs dry fast.

**I haven’t shipped a product yet myself, so I’m not writing this as someone with a success story to brag about.** I went digging through founder interviews, growth case studies, and the early playbooks of companies that actually made it, because before I build anything, I want to know how the distribution side works. This is what I found.

**10 methods**, most of them aimed squarely at reaching buyers in the US and other Tier 1 markets, since that’s where SaaS spending is concentrated right now.

One honest thing is… Most produce nothing for the first month, which is exactly where most people give up, right before things would start moving.

## Know Who You’re Building For First

## 1\. Build in Public on X (Formerly Twitter)

If reaching a US audience is the goal, X seems to be the strongest platform for it. Instagram and YouTube tend to serve your content to whatever audience you already have, which for most creators outside the US means a non-US audience by default. X behaves differently: post content that resonates with American interests and the algorithm tends to actually distribute it there.

The mechanics are simple. Set up a clean profile with the product link in the bio. Follow the people in your target audience.Then post three to five times a day, and frame those posts as “build in public” updates: a new feature just shipped, a payment flow just wired up, a bug just fixed.

**It’s basically vlogging in text form.** People follow the journey from empty repo to first sale, and some end up becoming early customers simply because they watched the thing get built.

> [**Lemon Squeezy**](https://x.com/jrfarr/status/2052754998409433393) is the classic example here, the founder built a real following on X, and that’s where the first wave of paying customers came from.

There’s also an actual “Build in Public” community on the platform, complete with a verified account running weekly threads like “what are you working on this week.” It’s one of the more reliable places to pick up your first few hundred impressions.

## 2\. The Reply Strategy

A brand new account with zero followers barely gets any reach, no matter how often you post. Ten, maybe fifteen views per tweet, that’s just normal at the start.

Replying to bigger accounts is the workaround.

It works the same way commenting works on Instagram. If a creator with a large following posts something that gets thousands of views, replies underneath get a slice of that traffic too. Reply early and reply well, and people will click through to check out your profile.

The process: find twenty to thirty accounts that your target audience already follows (in our example, big YouTube-focused creators), turn on notifications for all of them, and reply within minutes of anything they post. Speed seems to matter more than almost anything else here, the earlier a reply lands, the more eyes see it.

> [Taplio](https://www.facebook.com/groups/saasfoundersclub/posts/3166819517041143/), the LinkedIn content tool, reportedly built its early user base largely by replying to big creators’ tweets before anyone else got there first.

## 3\. One-on-One DM Outreach (The 1,000 Users Method)

This used to mean weeks of manual list-building. AI tools have made it much faster to pull off.

The idea: collect contact details, emails, Instagram handles, whatever’s available, for a thousand people who match your target profile.

Giving it away costs something, but it buys two things that are hard to get otherwise. First, you find out fast whether the product actually solves anything. Second, it builds a funnel almost by accident: message a thousand people, maybe two hundred reply, and a real chunk of those two hundred end up paying once they see the product genuinely helps them.

> [Superhuman](https://review.firstround.com/superhuman-onboarding-playbook/) is the case study most people cite here, the email client reportedly hit $75,000 in monthly recurring revenue in year one almost entirely through one-on-one onboarding calls with early users.

## 4\. Reddit: Community First, Pitch Never

Reddit runs on subreddits, and your target audience almost certainly has one. For YouTubers, that’s communities built around content creation and video editing. The rule that matters most: don’t pitch directly.

Reddit users spot a sales pitch from a mile away, and the backlash hits harder there than almost anywhere else.

## 5\. The Product Hunt Launch

Product Hunt works like an App Store for software, ranked by upvotes. Cracking the top five or six spots on launch day brings real traffic.

The trick is stacking this with the DM outreach strategy above. The two hundred people already using a free version of the product can be asked to upvote the launch. Even a modest follow-through rate gives a real shot at the front page, and the audience on that site is heavily US-based and tends to convert.

> [Notion got](https://www.producthunt.com/stories/how-notion-fostered-an-avid-community-on-product-hunt) good response from Product Hunt launch alone.

## AppSumo and Other Directory Listings

Same logic as Product Hunt: list on as many relevant directories as possible. Each one is low effort and adds a bit more surface area for discovery.

## 6\. Indie Hackers Journey Posts

Indie Hackers is essentially Build in Public in long-form article format instead of tweets. Founders post their wins, their failures, even raw revenue numbers, and the community actually engages with it instead of just scrolling past. Plausible Analytics, the privacy-focused analytics tool, points to a good chunk of its climb toward a million in annual revenue as coming straight out of posts and discussions on Indie Hackers.

## 7\. Hacker News Comments

Functionally the same as the X reply strategy: show up, comment thoughtfully on relevant threads, engage daily. There’s not much more nuance to it than that.

## 8\. Intent-Based Cold Outreach

This one’s sharper than generic cold outreach, because it goes after people already complaining about the exact problem the product solves. Search X, Reddit, and similar platforms for posts where the target audience is venting about a relevant pain point, then jump into the replies or DM them with a specific offer.

Finding these posts manually is tedious, so this is a good place to hand the search itself to AI: have it scan for relevant complaints from the target audience, then do the actual outreach by hand. Close CRM reportedly built much of its early customer base on this exact tactic.

## 9\. Micro-Influencer Collabs in Your Niche

This is the one method on the list that costs money, and it seems worth the spend. Creators with somewhere between 1,000 and 10,000 subscribers usually aren’t getting brand deals or meaningful ad revenue yet, which makes them more open to a collaboration.

## 10\. Directory Listings for Passive SEO

Listing on AI tool directories, like There’s An AI For That, Futurepedia, and Toolify, won’t flood anything with traffic on its own, but these listings compound over time, especially paired with ongoing blog content and basic SEO work.

## Where I’d Actually Start

Of the ten, four seem to matter more than the rest combined: **building in public on X, the reply strategy,** **direct DM outreach,** and **start creating content on LinkedIn it has massive audience which opens the doors to work with direct founders**,**and sometimes if your product seems interesting they will reach to you**.

If I were starting from zero, with no product built yet, those are the four I’d commit to first.

The uncomfortable part is that none of this produces anything in week one. Most of it produces silence for the first month, which is exactly where most people quit, right before things would start moving. That’s worth knowing going in, before the building even starts.
