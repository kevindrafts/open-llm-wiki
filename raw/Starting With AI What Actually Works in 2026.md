#### 1\. Your network beats your content strategy

The most successful clients I’ve worked with didn’t start with content. They started with the people they already knew.

Local shops. Former colleagues. A list of companies they’d worked with before. Small businesses in their existing network who trusted them already. They went to those people first, offered to solve a real problem with AI, and did the first projects for free or cheap.

Then something predictable happened. The free work turned into testimonials, the testimonials into paid projects, and after a while the referrals started coming on their own. AI sat on top of all of it as the amplifier. The relationships were the actual foundation.

Compare that to the standard advice: build an audience from zero, post daily, wait for inbound. That path works for some people, but it takes years. Your existing network can convert in weeks.

And if you’re wondering who to serve: Alex built his whole business on people in their 40s to early 60s, the demographic he calls lost and forgotten. Companies won’t invest in upskilling them because retirement is ten years out, yet they hold all the management knowledge and run the actual businesses. They don’t need to become technical. They need enough clarity to know what’s doable and to ask their juniors better questions. If that’s who’s in your network, you’re sitting on the most underserved market in AI education.

→ Action for today: write down 10 people or businesses you already know who have a problem you could solve with AI. Message one of them.

#### 2\. Do the first projects free, on purpose

Alex called this the Hormozi playbook, and he was upfront that he didn’t invent it: start doing the thing you want to charge for, but for free. Take a few people who are willing to go with you. Those people become your first testimonials, and nobody pays without testimonials. He did the same thing himself before charging for anything.

He put it more bluntly than I would have: you’re probably not ready to be a consultant if you haven’t consulted 10 people. What you used to do at work is not automatically the new job you’re selling.

There’s a second reason to do this, and it’s the uncomfortable one. Free projects test whether you even like the work. Alex sees ex-corporate people who loved consulting when they had 30 interns, then discover they hate being the one making the slides. Better to find that out on a free project than after you’ve built a website around it.

And when you do validate, watch the right signal. Alex comes from finance and he trusts purchase signals over everything else. Likes, comments, open rates, subscriber growth, all vanity. Someone putting their credit card down is the only proof that you’re solving a real problem. If nobody pays, the problem was never your landing page or your list size. You weren’t solving anything.

Do two or three free projects, get the testimonials in writing, then never work free again.

#### 3\. When barriers drop, taste goes up

This came up near the end of the session and it’s the point we kept circling back to.

The barriers to entry are collapsing. Anyone can set up agents, generate content, build a product in a weekend. Alex’s take: shortcuts are allowed, but they get arbitraged away. The market saturates with everyone chasing the same hack and the gains vanish. What can’t be copied is your personality, your taste, and your discipline, because you are the person doing the thing.

He’s honest about how slow the real path is. It took him more than seven months to get his first 10 paid subscribers. Seven months, from someone who now runs a media company. The creator economy has an exponential success curve, a few people doing millions while most do thousands, and what separates the top from the rest in his experience is consistency and whether you actually fit your niche.

He told a story about one of the most inspiring creators he’s met: a retired British lady who started a Substack about her garden. What she was planting, which seeds she bought, what she learned buying chairs. It sounds trivial and it found a real audience, because the fit between her and her topic was perfect. Apparently there’s a term for it now, creator-market fit. She could technically have made faceless YouTube shorts for kids instead. She would have quit in a month.

Taste is knowing which of the 50 things you could ship is the one worth shipping. Or recognizing when the AI output is technically fine but flat. And as far as I can tell it only develops one way: you make things, you compare them against work you admire, and you stay honest about the gap.

When everyone has the same tools, the tools stop being the differentiator. That was true before AI too. AI just made the gap visible much faster.

#### 4\. Set your AI up to argue with you

One practical thing from the Q&A, because it changes everything about working with these tools.

By default, AI confirms whatever you say. Show it your business plan and it will tell you the plan is great. Show it your draft and it will find things to praise. Left alone, it becomes a machine for telling you that you’re the greatest person alive, which feels nice and helps you exactly zero.

So instruct it to be critical. Here’s the kind of instruction I add:

Be constructive and critical. Challenge my assumptions.  
If my idea has a weakness, name it directly instead of  
softening it. Do not just confirm what I say. I want  
useful pushback, not validation.

Alex added an important caveat: even “be brutally critical with me” only goes so far, because these systems are designed to satisfy you. His fix is in the usage, not just the prompt. Use AI as an extractor, not an oracle. Have it **interview** **you**. You put in the context, you call the shots, and the AI pulls the answers out of you, because 99% of the time you already have them. Don’t ask it “how do I make a million dollars” and expect a real path back.

His favorite low-tech version: call a friend who knows you, have the AI generate the 10 interview questions, record the call, then let AI systematize what you said. You open up more with a friend than with a chat window.

Someone in the audience asked which model to use for this. We both landed in the same place: the model matters less than the setup. I work mostly with Claude and Claude Code, but an older model with the right instructions will challenge you better than the newest one set to agree with everything.

#### What this looks like in practice

Strip away the noise and the 2026 playbook is short:

1. List the people and businesses you already know
2. Offer to solve one real problem, free if needed
3. Collect the testimonial, raise your price
4. Validate with purchase signals, not likes
5. Use AI to amplify the delivery while you keep the relationship personal
6. Configure your AI to interview you and push back on you
7. Develop taste by shipping and comparing

You can start all of this without a huge audience. The people winning right now are mostly the ones who knew what they had, used their network, and put AI on top.

And if you feel behind: Alex’s closing words were that you’re probably not. Most of the world is still running on Excel, PowerPoint, and Gmail. If you have a paid AI subscription and have done a tenth of what’s in this post, you’re already in the top one percent of AI users.

Feel 10 percent of the FOMO, enough to keep learning. Skip the other 90.

Your 10-minute action: open your contacts, find one person whose business you understand, and send them a message asking what their most annoying recurring task is. That conversation is worth more than your next ten posts.
