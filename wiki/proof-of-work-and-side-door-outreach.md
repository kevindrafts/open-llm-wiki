Proof-of-work and side-door outreach treat opportunities as search problems: instead of waiting for a formal opening, a person or founder identifies a real human with a real problem, creates a small artifact that makes their usefulness legible, and combines specific outbound with public inbound signals that travel ahead of them.

# Proof-of-Work and Side-Door Outreach

## Core Thesis

The side-door source reframes jobs and opportunities as bundles of problems someone will pay or take a bet to solve. The public job post or obvious sales channel is only the front door. Side doors emerge when a person notices a problem, studies the context, and shows useful proof before asking for a formal opportunity.

The same pattern applies to careers, consulting, agency sales, and founder-led distribution:

- Find a person, company, or market you genuinely understand.
- Identify a problem, bottleneck, or opportunity not fully captured by a formal request.
- Create a small proof artifact: memo, teardown, prototype, dataset, analysis, guide, video, or specific cold email.
- Make the next step easy for the recipient.
- Build public artifacts so aligned people can discover how you think.

This connects to [[distribution-led-ai-startups]] and [[cold-email-deliverability-2026]] because specificity, timing, and proof are stronger than generic automation.

## Outbound and Inbound

Side doors have two modes:

- Outbound: direct, specific outreach to a person or company whose work you have studied.
- Inbound: public work that makes your thinking findable by the people who care about the same problem.

Good outbound does not ask the recipient to infer why you matter. It brings context, evidence, and a clear reason. Good inbound is not generic personal branding. It is a trail of useful artifacts that demonstrate taste, obsession, and ability.

Examples from the source include a specific email to Calm's CEO, a cold message that led to an internship that did not formally exist, an essay that led to a role, an AI research guide that led to a cofounder opportunity, and a public internship ask that reached Shopify.

## Practical Use

For builders and service providers, a side-door motion can look like:

- A teardown of a prospect's onboarding, SEO, ads, support flow, or landing page.
- A small prototype that demonstrates the proposed improvement.
- A dataset or analysis that reveals a market opportunity.
- A concise memo responding to a founder's public strategy or product note.
- A public guide that attracts people facing the same problem.

The pattern is high-effort and low-volume by design. The advantage comes from being more specific, useful, and legible than the mass of AI-polished generic applications and pitches.
